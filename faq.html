Corporate & Commercial Law

Company registration in Kenya is handled through the Business Registration Service (BRS) portal. You will need to reserve a company name, prepare a Memorandum and Articles of Association, appoint directors and a company secretary, and file the registration documents. We handle the full process on your behalf, including advising on the most appropriate structure — private limited company, public limited company, limited liability partnership, or branch office.

We provide end-to-end M&A support including legal due diligence, structuring the transaction, drafting and negotiating sale and purchase agreements, shareholder agreements, and regulatory filings with the Competition Authority of Kenya and the Capital Markets Authority where applicable. We have advised on acquisitions involving as much as 75% of issued share capital and have structured joint ventures for major Kenyan companies.

Litigation & Arbitration

Civil cases in Kenya can take anywhere from one to several years depending on the complexity of the matter, the court's workload, and whether parties cooperate with procedural timelines. The High Court typically takes longer than the Magistrates' Court. Given these delays, we always explore alternative dispute resolution — mediation or arbitration — with our clients, as these can resolve disputes significantly faster and at lower cost.

In mediation, a neutral third party helps disputing parties reach a voluntary settlement — the mediator does not impose a decision. In arbitration, an arbitrator (or panel) hears both sides and issues a binding award, similar to a court judgment but usually faster and more private. Both are alternatives to litigation. We advise clients on which option suits their situation and represent them through either process.

Under the Land Acquisition Act and the Constitution of Kenya 2010, the government may acquire private land for public purposes but must pay prompt and full compensation. You have the right to be notified, to challenge the valuation, and to receive fair market value for your land. If you believe the offered compensation is inadequate, you can appeal to the Environment and Land Court. We are currently handling a Ksh. 272 Million compulsory acquisition matter and have deep experience in this area.

Property & Real Estate

Conveyancing in Kenya involves a title search at the lands registry, drafting and reviewing the sale agreement, carrying out due diligence on the property, facilitating payment and transfer of title, and registration of the new owner. With the introduction of e-title deeds by the National Land Commission, additional digital verification steps are now required. We guide buyers and sellers through every stage to ensure a clean, legally sound transfer.

A properly drafted commercial lease should cover the lease term and renewal options, rent amount and review mechanism, permitted use of the premises, repair and maintenance obligations, assignment and subletting rights, break clauses, service charges, insurance obligations, and dispute resolution provisions. We draft and review commercial leases for both landlords and tenants, ensuring your interests are protected throughout the tenancy.

Land disputes in Kenya are handled by the Environment and Land Court. Before filing a case, we recommend attempting negotiation or mediation, which can be faster and less expensive. If litigation is necessary, we will carry out a title search, review survey maps, and build the strongest possible case based on the documentary evidence. We advise acting quickly as delay can complicate your position.

Employment & Labour Law

If you believe you have been unfairly dismissed, you may file a claim with the Employment and Labour Relations Court. Under the Employment Act, a dismissal is unfair if it is not for a valid reason or if the correct procedure was not followed. You generally have three years from the date of dismissal to file a claim. Remedies include reinstatement, compensation, or both. We advise acting quickly and preserving all written communications with your employer.

Under the Employment Act, an employment contract must include the employer's and employee's details, the job title and description, the date of commencement, the place of work, hours of work, remuneration and benefits, leave entitlements, notice periods, and termination procedures. We draft and review employment contracts for both employers and employees, ensuring full compliance with Kenyan employment law and protecting your interests.

Under the Employment Act, an employer must give at least one month's notice (or pay in lieu) before making an employee redundant, notify the labour officer and the union (if applicable), and pay a severance allowance of at least 15 days' pay for each completed year of service. Failure to follow this procedure can give rise to a claim for unfair termination. If you believe your redundancy was not genuine, we can advise on challenging the decision.
